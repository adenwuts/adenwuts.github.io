Hence we have transformed our data from the time domain to a frequency domain. The above spectrum is 'static' since it gives a static view of the frequency over time (i.e. the spectrum doesn't change with time). We can also include time in a "dynamic" spectrum, where "power" is shown through colour.

What questions to ask when looking at time frequency data

  1. Determine what is being shown in the plot. Power, connectivity, correlation with behavior, etc.
  2. Inspect the ranges and limits of the plot. What are the x- and y-axes? Color scale? Is activity cut off at the edges?
  3. Look at the results. Distributed or localised? Why only this electrode or time/frequency window?
  4. Link the results to the experiment design. What is time=0? Multiple task events?
  5. Understand the statistical procedures. Thresholding? Multiple comparisons addressed? Exploratory?

  2. Ranges and limits: Symetric colour plot with 0 at green. Approximately 1.25 seconds of time represented in the time axis. Lowest frequency ~1Hz and top of 40Hz.
  3. Look at the results: Looking for localised vs. distributed features (e.g. blue feature is distributed over time but localised over frequency; Red features are "band-limited" and very narrow in frequency range). Should also examine if any features are cut off by limits.
  4. Link the results to experiment design.
  5. Understand statistical procedures: No evidence of statistical procedures used on plot. This means we need to be qualitative in analysis, since we can't be sure which features are noise vs. information.
